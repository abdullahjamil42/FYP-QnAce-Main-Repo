# Technical Interview Rubric

## Overview
Technical questions assess domain knowledge, problem-solving process, and
the ability to communicate complex ideas clearly under pressure.

---

## Scoring Criteria

### Excellent (85–100)
- Correctly identifies the core problem or concept before diving into details.
- Demonstrates structured thinking — breaks the problem into sub-problems.
- Uses precise technical vocabulary appropriate to the domain.
- Acknowledges trade-offs (e.g., time vs. space, consistency vs. availability).
- Provides concrete examples, references real tools/frameworks/algorithms.
- Discusses edge cases, failure modes, or scalability considerations.
- Communicates clearly — no jargon overload; adapts to interviewer cues.

### Average (50–70)
- Identifies the problem area but skips reasoning steps.
- Uses correct terminology but explanations stay surface-level.
- Provides a solution but doesn't discuss alternatives or trade-offs.
- May handle the happy path but overlooks edge cases.
- Communication is understandable but lacks structure.

### Poor (0–40)
- Misidentifies the problem or jumps to an incorrect solution.
- Uses incorrect terminology or conflates concepts.
- No structured approach — stream-of-consciousness response.
- Cannot articulate trade-offs when prompted.
- Response is too short or overly verbose without substance.

---

## Example Questions
- "Explain how you would design a URL shortening service."
- "Walk me through how a database index improves query performance."
- "What are the trade-offs between microservices and a monolith?"

## Key Evaluation Signals
| Signal | Weight | Notes |
|--------|--------|-------|
| Conceptual accuracy | 30% | Correct understanding of fundamentals |
| Structured reasoning | 25% | Breaks down problem, considers alternatives |
| Trade-off analysis | 20% | Acknowledges pros/cons of approaches |
| Communication clarity | 15% | Explains complex ideas simply |
| Depth & examples | 10% | Real-world references, edge cases |
